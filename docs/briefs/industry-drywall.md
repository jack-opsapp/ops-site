## Industry Profile

### Market Size
- U.S. drywall and insulation installers industry: **$122.2 billion** (2026)
- **127,000 businesses** in the United States (declining at CAGR of 0.9%)
- Global drywall contractor market: **$47.8 billion** (2023), projected to reach **$68.2 billion** by 2033 at 3.6% CAGR
- North America drywall market: **$19.16 billion** (2025), growing at 5.70% CAGR
- Market driven by new construction, renovations, and growing retrofitting demand across aging commercial buildings

### Industry Segments
- **Small drywall crew / subcontractor (majority):** 2-5 workers, $100K-$500K revenue
- **Mid-sized company:** 5-15 workers, $500K-$2M revenue
- **Established operation:** 15-50+ workers, $2M-$10M+ revenue
- **Specialty segments:** acoustic ceilings, fire-rated assemblies, decorative finishes
- **Residential vs. commercial split:** residential new construction, residential renovation, commercial new build, commercial tenant improvement
- Most drywall contractors operate as **subcontractors** to general contractors

### Service Pricing & Revenue
- Median drywall installer salary: **$58,140/year** (May 2024 BLS data)
- Median hourly wage: **$27.41/hour** (2025)
- Drywall contractor average: **$24.05/hour** ($50,000/year)
- Regional variation: Hawaii ($66,100) to Alabama ($36,300)
- Drywall installer wages growing **11%** vs. 7.3% overall construction median
- Residential drywall: $1.50-$3.50 per sq ft installed (hang + finish)
- Commercial drywall: $2.00-$5.00 per sq ft installed (depending on specifications)
- Typical residential project: $2,000-$15,000
- Typical commercial project: $10,000-$500,000+

### Project Characteristics
- **Multi-phase workflow:** hanging, taping, first coat, second coat, sanding, finishing
- **Drying time between phases:** 24-48 hours between mud coats (weather/humidity dependent)
- **Trade dependencies:** must follow electrical/plumbing rough-in, must precede painting
- **Quality is visible:** drywall finishing quality is immediately apparent -- poor work shows through paint
- **Piece-rate payment** common: many installers paid per sheet hung or per square foot finished
- **Dust and mess:** sanding phases require protection of other trades' work and customer property

### Technology Adoption
- Construction management platforms (Procore, Buildertrend) used by GCs but not adopted by most drywall subs
- General FSM tools (Jobber, Housecall Pro) handle scheduling but don't understand phased workflows
- Drywall-specific estimating tools exist (FieldGroove) but lack comprehensive business management
- Most small drywall companies still operate on phone calls, text messages, and handshake agreements
- Adoption barrier: drywall crews are often on dusty, messy job sites -- tools must be rugged and simple
- Mobile-first tools gaining traction but most platforms are desktop-centric

### Labor Situation
- **56% of contractors report failing to meet schedule timelines** due to labor shortages
- Drywall installers are **one of the hardest skilled labor roles to fill**
- **88% of firms with craft workers report job openings**
- U.S. construction industry needed **439,000 additional workers** in 2025
- Installer wages growing **11%** -- among the largest increases in construction
- Labor shortage driving contractors to expand crew sizes, increase supervision, or rely more on sub-crews
- Quality issues increasing as less experienced workers fill gaps

## Pain Points (3 Blocks)

### PAIN POINT 1: FIVE PHASES, THREE TRADES, AND ZERO ROOM FOR ERROR
**forLine:** "For drywall contractors managing hanging, taping, mudding, sanding, and finishing across multiple job sites while coordinating with electricians, plumbers, and painters"

**Bullets:**
1. **Drywall work is inherently multi-phase.** Hanging, taping, first mud coat, second coat, sanding, finishing -- each phase requires specific skills, drying time (24-48 hours between coats), and sequential scheduling. A delay in one phase cascades through the entire project.
2. **Trade coordination is the job within the job.** Electrical and plumbing rough-in must be complete before hanging starts. Inspection must pass before finishing begins. Painters can't start until you're done. Missed handoffs between trades halt progress and cost everyone money.
3. **Multi-site scheduling multiplies the complexity.** A 5-crew drywall company might have crews at 3-4 different job sites, each at different phases. Knowing which crew goes where tomorrow -- and whether the site is actually ready for their phase -- requires real-time visibility that phone calls and text messages can't provide.
4. **Weather and humidity affect drying times.** Mud doesn't cure on a fixed schedule. High humidity extends drying time. If you schedule sanding based on standard timing and the compound isn't dry, your crew shows up and can't work -- wasted labor on a day you could have sent them to another site.

**Supporting evidence:**
- 56% of contractors report failing to meet schedule timelines
- Missed handoffs between trades create bottlenecks that halt progress
- Trade coordination is "exponentially more difficult because they're working with hundreds of different scenarios"
- As experience levels drop, site activity becomes harder to organize and sequencing falls behind

### PAIN POINT 2: THE LABOR CRISIS IS MAKING EVERY OTHER PROBLEM WORSE
**forLine:** "For drywall company owners who can't find skilled hangers and finishers while wages climb 11% and quality drops with every inexperienced hire"

**Bullets:**
1. **Drywall installers are among the hardest skilled labor roles to fill in construction.** 88% of firms with craft workers report open positions. The U.S. construction industry needed 439,000 additional workers in 2025 -- and drywall is one of the most affected trades.
2. **Wages are climbing 11% while margins stay flat.** Drywall installer median wages have grown 11%, compared to 7.3% for overall construction. If your estimates use last year's labor rates, every job is less profitable than projected.
3. **Inexperienced workers create quality issues that weren't a problem five years ago.** Drywall finishing quality is immediately visible -- poor mudding and sanding shows through paint. What once required basic quality checks now requires constant supervision, adding management overhead.
4. **Adding more workers doesn't solve the problem -- it creates new ones.** Overcrowded sites, higher safety risk, less accountability, and more scheduling complexity. The answer isn't more bodies -- it's making the crews you have more productive.

**Supporting evidence:**
- Drywall installer wages growing 11% vs. 7.3% overall construction median
- 88% of firms employing craft workers report job openings
- "Drywall installation, once reliable enough to pass inspection immediately, can require an entire day of corrections"
- Adding more workers can lead to "overcrowded sites, higher safety risk, and less accountability"

### PAIN POINT 3: ENTERPRISE SOFTWARE FOR SUBCONTRACTOR BUDGETS
**forLine:** "For drywall subcontractors paying GC prices for software they only use 20% of"

**Bullets:**
1. **Procore and Buildertrend are built for general contractors, not subs.** They offer comprehensive project management, but drywall subs don't need bidding portals, architectural drawing management, or client selection tools. They need crew scheduling, phase tracking, and invoicing.
2. **ServiceTitan costs $245-$500+ per technician per month** with $5,000-$50,000 implementation. For a 10-person drywall crew, that's $30,000-$60,000/year in software. Most drywall subs would laugh at that number.

4. **The result: most drywall companies use nothing.** They operate on phone calls, text messages, and handshake agreements with GCs. The software market has failed this segment by offering either too much (Procore) or too little (Jobber) at prices that don't match sub margins.

### SOLUTION 1: PHASE-BY-PHASE SCHEDULING ACROSS EVERY JOB SITE
**painPointRef:** Five Phases, Three Trades, and Zero Room for Error

**Copy direction:** Schedule hanging crew at Site A Monday, taping crew at Site B, and finishing crew at Site C -- with phase completion tracking that shows which sites are ready for the next step.
