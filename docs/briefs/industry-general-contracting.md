## 3. PAIN POINTS

### PAIN POINT 1: Subcontractor Coordination Chaos

The average GC juggles 2-8 active projects simultaneously, each with multiple trades sequenced precisely. Coordinating via text messages, phone calls, and spreadsheets is the industry norm — and it is breaking people.

**Proof Point 1 — The numbers are damning:**
- 54% of contractors blame delays on poor coordination between subcontractors
- 70% say poor jobsite coordination causes cost overruns and schedule blowouts
- Poor communication causes one-third of all construction project failures (PMI)

**Proof Point 2 — The daily reality is unsustainable:**
> "Ask any contractor about their worst day on the job, and you'll likely hear about a scheduling disaster — subcontractors showing up with no materials, concrete trucks arriving before the forms are ready, or critical path delays cascading into weeks of setbacks."

From ContractorTalk forums, GCs list their biggest sub pain points as: "Prompt response to quote solicitations, return calls and emails promptly, giving realistic promises regarding schedule and following through accordingly."

**Proof Point 3 — Project managers drowning in coordination overhead:**
- PMs negotiate with **30-40 subs** across a couple dozen trades per project
- **35% of construction professionals' time** spent on non-productive activities
- Over **14 hours per week** wasted on "looking for project information, resolving conflicts, and managing mistakes and rework"

**Proof Point 4 — Different schedules = everything breaks:**
> "When office teams, field crews, and subcontractors operate from different versions of the schedule, communication breaks down."
> "Subcontractors live in the chaos between jobsite expectations and crew availability."

### PAIN POINT 2: Enterprise Software Not Built for $3M Operations

The GC software market has a massive gap. Procore and Buildertrend are either built for enterprise operations or carry enterprise-grade complexity. Small GCs with 2-10 employees are caught in a dead zone.

**Proof Point 1 — Procore is priced out of reach:**
- Annual cost starts around $4,500-$10,000/year; most pay $20,000-$60,000/year
- Revenue-based pricing (0.1-0.2% of annual construction volume) means costs scale with success
- Renewal increases of 5-14% per year

> "Procore was overkill for our team."
> "It is not for a small company and a beginner. Beginners get overwhelmed."
> "If you're a small account, you might not get the same attention as enterprise clients paying $50K+ per year."

**Proof Point 2 — Buildertrend's learning curve kills adoption:**
- Plans range from $199-$900+/month, with $400-$1,500 onboarding costs
- Per-user fees of $39/month beyond initial 3-user allocation

> "It's a hard product to learn how to use, and there is like 10x more clicking than there needs to be."
> "They also don't give you any time to learn the system before you have to pay the subscription."

**Proof Point 3 — Data lock-in traps small GCs:**
> "Once information is inside their system, retrieving it later is a massive challenge... there is no simple or bulk way to download years' worth of files, photos, proposals, and customer information."

**Proof Point 4 — 70% have no technology roadmap:**
- 70% of contractors have no formal technology roadmap (2024)
- Nearly two-thirds cite uncertain payback periods (often exceeding 24 months) as chief deterrent
- Small firms allocate only 1-5% of annual revenue to tech

### PAIN POINT 3: Cash Flow Strangulation & Payment Tracking

Construction payment cycles are brutal. GCs front labor, materials, and sub payments while waiting 45-90 days for draws. This is the #1 killer of construction businesses.

**Proof Point 1 — Payment delays have exploded:**
- 82% of contractors now face payment waits over 30 days (up from 49% two years prior)
- GCs believe payments occur in 30 days; subcontractors actually wait **56 days on average**
- Payment delays have driven up industry costs by an estimated **$280 billion**

**Proof Point 2 — Cash flow kills more construction companies than anything else:**
- 96% of construction companies fail before year 10 — cash flow is the primary driver
- 56% of contractors have turned down projects due to cash-flow risks
- 43% of subcontractors report insufficient working capital for unexpected expenses

**Proof Point 3 — The math is terrifying for small GCs:**
- A $3.7M revenue GC billing $300K/month with a 47-day collection cycle has **$470K+ outstanding** at any time
- That's money earned, costs already paid, but cash not yet received

**Proof Point 4 — Rework compounds the problem:**
- **30% of all work on construction sites is rework**
- Poor communication and bad data account for **48% of all rework**
- Rework costs the US construction industry **$177 billion annually**

## 4. COMPETITOR ANALYSIS

### COMPETITOR 1: Buildertrend

**Pricing:**
- Essential: $199/mo (introductory; was $499)
- Advanced: $499/mo (introductory; was $799)
- Complete: $900+/mo
- Onboarding: $400-$1,500
- Per-user overage: $39/mo beyond 3 users
- Payment processing: 2.99% + $0.30 + $1.49 platform fee
- **Typical Year 1 cost:** $6,500-$7,500

**What GCs Love:**
- Automatic homeowner notifications
- Scheduling and client portal features
- Most teams get comfortable in a few days (when they survive onboarding)

**What GCs Hate:**
- Steep learning curve with excessive clicking
- Clunky estimating tools
- "Great for schedules and client comms — less so for cost control"
- No data export — bulk download impossible, creating vendor lock-in
- Performance issues: slow load times, crashes
- Pricing nearly doubles after introductory period
- No free trial

**Where It Fails 2-10 Person Ops:**
- Feature bloat, cost scales with per-user fees, no offline mode, no Spanish support

### COMPETITOR 2: Procore

**Pricing:**
- Revenue-based tied to Annual Construction Volume (ACV)
- Small contractors: $4,500-$10,000/year minimum
- Mid-size: $20,000-$60,000/year
- Large: $100,000-$600,000+/year
- Annual contracts required (sometimes multi-year)
- Renewal increases: 5-14%/year
- **No published pricing — requires sales call**

**What GCs Love:**
- Comprehensive project management
- Document control and drawing management
- Strong mobile app
- Unlimited users on each plan

**What GCs Hate:**
- Prohibitively expensive for small operations
- Overwhelming complexity for simple needs
- "Almost every action requires a full page reload"
- Poor support for small accounts
- Timesheet loading takes "almost 5 minutes for one week"

> "Procore is prohibitively expensive and, while marketed to specialty contractors, is very clearly geared to general contractors."
> "After dozens of emails to all contacts... received not ONE response except for invoices."

### Competitive Landscape

| Competitor | Target | Pricing | Weakness for Small GC |
|-----------|--------|---------|----------------------|
| Procore | Enterprise GCs ($50M+) | $4,500-$600K+/yr | Too expensive, too complex |
| Buildertrend | Residential builders | $199-$900+/mo | Learning curve, data lock-in |
| Contractor Foreman | Small contractors | $49+/mo | Limited depth, basic |
| Fieldwire | Field teams | Freemium | Limited project management |
| JobTread | Small-mid builders | Mid-range | Newer, less established |
| **OPS** | Field crews 2-50 | **$79/mo flat** | Newer entrant |

## 5. OPS DIFFERENTIATORS FOR GENERAL CONTRACTING

### $79/mo Flat Pricing Disrupts Per-User Models

- **Buildertrend:** $199-$900/mo + $39/user beyond 3 seats. A GC with 8 team members pays $394/mo minimum
- **Procore:** $4,500-$60,000+/year depending on volume
- **OPS:** $79/mo flat. Period

For a typical NAHB builder doing $3.7M in revenue, OPS costs **0.026% of annual revenue** vs. Procore's 0.1-0.2%.

| "Where am I going, what am I doing" | Multi-module project dashboards |
| Quick crew communication | Document control systems |
| Basic job costing visibility | Enterprise financial management |
| Works on a phone at a job site | Works best on a desktop in an office |
| $79/mo they can budget for | $4,500-$60,000/year |
| Download today, use tomorrow | 2-week to 12-month implementation |
