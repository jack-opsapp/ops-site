- **The U.S. electrical contracting industry is a $347.5 billion market with approximately 262,000 businesses, and 88% of establishments have fewer than 20 employees.** This is a massive addressable market dominated by small operators who are dramatically underserved by current FSM software, which is built for enterprise-scale operations.

No current FSM tool addresses these workflows well.

- **The labor shortage is acute: 80,000 new electricians needed annually, but only 7,000 enter while 10,000 retire each year.** Crew retention matters more than ever, and software that frustrates field workers directly impacts turnover. OPS's crew-first positioning is not just a marketing angle -- it is a workforce retention tool.

## 3. PAIN POINTS (3 Blocks, 4 Bullets Each)

### Pain Point 1: Scheduling Is a Multi-Dimensional Puzzle

**forLine:** "For electrical contractors juggling permits, inspections, and emergency calls"

- **Inspection chains create rigid dependencies.** Most electrical jobs require 3 separate inspections (cover, service, final), each of which must be scheduled with the municipality by 3 PM the previous day. A failed inspection cascades delays across every downstream job on the calendar. No mainstream FSM tool models these dependencies natively.
- **Emergency service calls destroy planned schedules daily.** Residential electricians report that service calls constantly disrupt construction project timelines. A crew planned for a panel upgrade gets pulled to a no-power emergency, and the original job slides with no automatic rescheduling.
- **Multi-day projects compound the problem.** Unlike HVAC service calls (show up, fix, leave), electrical work frequently spans multiple days with material deliveries, permit waits, and inspection gates between phases. Spreadsheets cannot represent this complexity, and basic FSM tools treat every job as a single-visit event.
- **Double-booking is rampant in basic tools.** Jobber users report the app "allows double booking without alerting me, and there's no simple way to mark individual blackout dates or times for my workers." When you have 6 technicians across 4 job sites, one double-book can cost an entire day of labor.

**Supporting Evidence:**
- "After spending a lot of time comparing multiple softwares, none of them does everything, and almost everything out there has at least one pretty big glaring technical missing piece." -- Electrician Talk forum user
- "One missed appointment or delayed crew can trigger lost time, frustrated customers, and backed-up projects." -- BuildOps resource article
- "Service calls, scheduling set backs, and unpredictable work flow" cited as top operational pain -- Electrician Talk forum

### Pain Point 2: Crew Coordination Breaks Down as You Grow

**forLine:** "For electrical shop owners managing 5-15 technicians across multiple sites"

- **Text-message scheduling creates chaos at scale.** Electricians report running crews through WhatsApp groups and individual texts, resulting in "as many as 100 messages a day" for project managers. A dispatcher updates a job scope on a sticky note that gets buried; the update never reaches the tech; the customer complains; the contractor eats the callback cost.
- **The 5-7 technician tipping point is where manual methods collapse.** Once businesses hit 5-7 techs, "spreadsheets and texts start breaking down, which is usually the tipping point where chaos starts costing real money." An electrician on Electrician Talk reported that "twice in one week crews went to the wrong address" while using Google Calendar and Slack.
- **Apprentice/journeyman pairing is a compliance requirement, not a preference.** Assigning crews without tracking license types and ratios creates regulatory exposure. States enforce different ratios, and public works projects have additional requirements. No mainstream FSM tool tracks crew composition compliance.
- **Field workers are the last to know when plans change.** Current tools are built for the dispatcher's screen, not the crew's phone. By the time a schedule change reaches the field through a chain of calls and texts, the crew has already driven to the wrong site or started the wrong task.

**Supporting Evidence:**
- "A dispatcher updates a job scope on a sticky note that gets buried, the update never reaches the technician, the tech does the original work, the customer complains, and now the contractor has to eat the cost of a callback." -- Big News Network article on electrical crew management
- "Texting itself is considered chaotic, with group chats not working well due to Android and iOS challenges." -- Procore jobsite communication research
- "Going from paper timesheets and material sheets to dedicated software has honestly saved our a$$." -- Electrician Talk forum user

### Pain Point 3: Software Costs Don't Fit Small Electrical Shops

**forLine:** "For 2-10 person electrical companies tired of paying enterprise prices"

- **ServiceTitan costs $245-500 per technician per month, requires 3+ technicians, and takes 3-12 months to implement.** For a 5-person electrical shop, that is $14,700-$30,000/year in software alone before implementation fees ($5K-$50K). One Capterra reviewer wrote: "It seem they are here to destroy small businesses to make more room for larger franchise."

GPS tracking is not included -- it requires FleetSharp at $25/vehicle/month extra. And the mobile app has sub-2.0 ratings on both iOS and Android app stores.

The Connect plan for 5 users is $169/month, and each user beyond the plan limit costs $29/month extra. Critical features like automated quote follow-ups are locked behind the $349/month Grow plan.
- **"Feature gating" means you pay for the plan but not the features you need.** Housecall Pro users report that the base plan is "reasonably priced, but GPS tracking, proposals, and the price book are separate add-ons that can push the monthly cost significantly higher than the advertised starting price." When human support was replaced with AI chatbots in 2025, users could no longer get help with billing issues.

If you need scheduling, dispatch, and crew coordination without the enterprise overhead, OPS provides a simpler path that your crew will actually use.

### Q5: What features should an electrician look for in a job management app?

**A:** Electrical contractors have unique needs that generic FSM tools often miss. Look for: (1) **Multi-day job support** -- electrical work spans days or weeks with permit holds and inspection gates, not just single service calls; (2) **Offline mode** -- you work in places with no signal, and your app should too; (3) **Crew-level visibility** -- your apprentices and journeymen need to see their schedule and job details, not just the dispatcher; (4) **Real-time sync** -- when a schedule changes, every crew member should know instantly, not through a chain of texts; (5) **Affordable pricing** -- if 42% of electrical firms generate less than $1M/year, software should not cost $15K-$30K annually; (6) **Fast setup** -- implementation timelines measured in minutes, not months.
