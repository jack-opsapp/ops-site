## Pain Points (3 Blocks)

### PAIN POINT 1: QUOTING TAKES DAYS, NOT MINUTES
**forLine:** "For glass company owners and office managers"

**Bullets:**
1. **The "let me call you back" problem**: When a customer calls for a quote, the answer is almost never immediate. Staff check spreadsheets, call suppliers for pricing, verify material availability, and manually calculate square footage. By the time the quote goes out, the customer has called a competitor.
2. **Manual measurement-to-quote pipeline**: A field tech drives out to measure, writes dimensions on paper, drives back to the shop, hands it to the office, who manually enters it into a spreadsheet or QuickBooks, then creates a quote. One glass shop documented this as a **5-hour process per quote** -- reduced to 1 hour after digitizing.
3. **Material complexity kills speed**: Every piece of glass has variables -- type (tempered, laminated, insulated, low-E), thickness, tint, edge finish, hardware. Quoting requires looking up each variable. Without a system, this is a manual pricing exercise every single time.
4. **Lost quotes = lost revenue**: Estimates created in the field that don't get approved quickly result in lost jobs. One owner described coming back to a quote "a week or two later" and losing the job entirely. Speed is the differentiator between winning and losing work.

**Supporting evidence:**
- Van Wert Glass Co: quoting time reduced from **2 days to minutes** after adopting software, saving **3.5 hours/week**
- Elmer Glass: quoting reduced from **5 hours to 1 hour** (80% improvement)
- Glazier Software claims **60% faster quoting** across their customer base
- Elmo Anywhere built their entire product around "first-call resolution" in auto glass

### PAIN POINT 2: SCHEDULING IS A WHITEBOARD AND A PRAYER
**forLine:** "For foremen and dispatchers at glass companies"

**Bullets:**
1. **Whiteboards, paper diaries, and verbal updates**: One glass company literally used a whiteboard with magnets for their entire scheduling operation, supplemented by verbal handoffs. When key people were away, things fell apart. This is not an outlier -- it is the norm in glass.
2. **Material-dependent scheduling**: You cannot install glass that has not arrived. Tempered glass takes 1-7 business days domestically (4-8+ weeks if imported). Scheduling must account for fabrication lead times, but most shops track this in their heads or on sticky notes.
3. **Emergency dispatch breaks the plan**: Board-up services for storm damage, break-ins, and vandalism are 24/7 operations. An emergency call at 2 AM means reshuffling the next day's schedule. Without a digital system, the dispatcher is calling everyone individually.
4. **Shop-to-field coordination gap**: Glass companies have two workforces -- shop crews (fabrication, cutting, tempering) and field crews (installation). A job is not ready for the field until the shop finishes. Tracking this handoff on paper means field crews show up to jobs where the glass is not ready.

**Supporting evidence:**
- Elmer Glass case study: whiteboard + magnets + verbal updates resulted in "miscommunication and missing data"
- Glass Magazine: "99% of problems in construction start from a lack of communication"
- GlassManager marketing: "scheduling is often a massive headache for glass shop staff, with information kept in different places, not kept up-to-date, or only editable by one person"
- Smart Glazier: scheduling may involve "Excel, a whiteboard in the office or even just a paper diary"

### PAIN POINT 3: FIELD ERRORS ARE EXPENSIVE AND UNRECOVERABLE
**forLine:** "For glass installation crews and field supervisors"

**Bullets:**
1. **Measurement errors mean recuts -- or total loss**: Tempered glass CANNOT be recut. A measurement error of a few millimeters on a tempered panel means scrapping the entire piece and starting over -- new material, new fabrication time, new delivery. For custom panels, this can cost hundreds of dollars per error.
2. **Paper job details get lost or garbled**: Job details collected on paper, then re-entered into another system, create a telephone game of errors. "If you have one tiny little piece of information not right, it will not export" (quoted from a GlassManager user at Elliott Glass).
3. **No photo documentation trail**: Insurance claims for storm damage, break-ins, and vandalism require before/during/after photos from multiple angles. Without a system that captures and attaches photos to jobs, crews take photos on personal phones that never get filed properly.
4. **Double entry wastes hours**: Information entered on a paper work order, then re-entered into accounting software, then re-entered into an invoicing tool. One case study documented that this "doubled the time required for quoting." Every hour spent on double entry is an hour not spent on billable work.

**Supporting evidence:**
- Synerglass-Soft: measurement errors are "one of the most frequent contributors to inefficiencies in glass cutting"
- GCCM Corp: "inaccurate measurements are one of the most common and costly mistakes in glass installation"
- CompanyCam: "Every insurance restoration claim needs three distinct sets of photos: initial damage, work in progress, and completed repairs"
- Waste minimization studies show systematic programs save **1% of total business turnover** -- for a $2M glass company, that is $20,000/year recovered

## FAQ Material (5 Questions)

### Q1: Do I really need glass-specific software, or can a general scheduling app work?
**Answer direction:** Glass companies have unique workflows that generic tools miss -- material-dependent scheduling (you cannot install unfinished tempered panels), shop-to-field handoffs, emergency board-up dispatch, and insurance photo documentation. But you also do not need a $95/user/month glass ERP if your core problem is scheduling and crew coordination. OPS gives you the field-level tools that matter -- scheduling, dispatch, job tracking, photo capture, crew communication -- without forcing you into a complex, glass-only system you will spend months learning. If you are currently using a whiteboard and QuickBooks, OPS is the bridge between "manual chaos" and "enterprise ERP you do not need."

### Q2: How do glass companies currently handle scheduling without software?
**Answer direction:** Most glass shops use some combination of a whiteboard in the office, a paper diary, Excel spreadsheets, or verbal handoffs between the office and field crews. Some use the calendar in QuickBooks. The problem is that these methods break when key people are absent, when emergency calls come in, or when material delivery dates shift. One documented case (Elmer Glass) used a whiteboard with magnets supplemented by verbal updates, resulting in constant miscommunication and missing job data. The industry average is far behind other trades in digital adoption.

### Q3: What does a glass company management app cost?
**Answer direction:** Glass-specific software ranges from $65 to $230+ per user per month (GlassManager Lite at $65, Professional at $95; Smart Glazier Business at $230/month). Most also charge per field user ($12/month at GlassManager) and require setup fees ($250-$495). Many, like Glazier Software, do not publish pricing at all and require a demo call. OPS is free to start with no credit card required and no per-field-user charges. For a 5-person glass company, traditional glass software costs $500-$1,500/month before you have even added your field crews. OPS lets you get started for free and scale as you grow.

### Q4: Can OPS handle both residential and commercial glass work?
**Answer direction:** Yes. Glass companies rarely do only one type of work. You might have a shower door installation in the morning, a commercial storefront measurement at lunch, and an emergency board-up call in the afternoon. OPS handles all job types through flexible scheduling and job tracking -- residential, commercial, auto glass, emergency services. Unlike glass-specific ERPs that are built for commercial contract glazing workflows (AIA billing, change orders, submittals), OPS focuses on the operational core that every glass job shares: who is going where, when, with what information, and how does the office know what happened.

### Q5: We are a small glass shop with 3-5 people. Is OPS overkill?
**Answer direction:** The opposite. Most glass software is overkill for small shops. GlassManager requires a minimum of 2-3 full users. Smart Glazier starts at $150/month. TRUE requires a demo and onboarding. These platforms are built for companies with 20+ employees and dedicated admin staff. OPS is built for teams that are too small to have a "back office" -- where the owner is also the estimator, the dispatcher, and sometimes the installer. Download it today, set up your crew, and start scheduling jobs. No implementation timeline. No training program. No consultant. If your biggest problem is "I have too much in my head and my whiteboard is full," OPS solves that on day one.
