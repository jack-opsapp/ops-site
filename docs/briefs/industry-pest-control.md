### 2. INDUSTRY PROFILE

#### Market Size and Structure

- **US pest control market revenue:** $26.1-28.5 billion (2025), projected $32.8B by 2028 at ~5.7% CAGR
- **Number of companies:** 32,720+ active businesses (some sources report 33,122)
- **Total employees:** ~147,000 (as of most recent data)
- **Revenue per employee:** $193,725
- **Average revenue per company:** ~$401,900; average per location: $1.6 million
- **Pest control software market:** Growing at 8.7% CAGR, projected $602.1M by 2030
- **Software adoption:** 65% of pest control companies use specialized FSM software (2026 est.); 82% use mobile apps for scheduling/routing; **~35% still operate without dedicated software**

#### Typical Company Profile (OPS Target)

- **Size:** 2-15 employees (the vast majority are SMBs under 50)
- **Technicians:** 2-8 field technicians running 8-12 stops/day each
- **Vehicles:** 2-6 service trucks/vans per company
- **Revenue:** $200K-$1.5M annually
- **Service mix:** 70% residential, 30% commercial (by revenue industry-wide)
- **Contract model:** Monthly ($35-75 residential, $100-500 commercial) or quarterly recurring
- **Customer churn:** 15-20% residential, 5-10% commercial annually

#### Key Workflow Characteristics Unique to Pest Control

1. **Route-based recurring service:** Unlike project-based trades, pest control runs the same routes weekly/monthly/quarterly. Route density (clustering stops geographically) is the primary profitability lever. Technicians average 10 stops/day.

2. **Seasonal demand surges:** Spring and summer bring 2-3x demand spikes (termite swarms in spring, mosquitoes/cockroaches/wasps in summer). Companies must rapidly scale scheduling, often hiring seasonal techs, then manage slow winter periods.

3. **Chemical tracking and EPA compliance:** Federal and state regulations require tracking EPA registration numbers, active ingredients, dilution rates, quantities applied, application methods, and applicator certification. Many states require records stored for up to 7 years. This is not optional -- it is a legal requirement.

4. **Residential vs. commercial contracts:** Commercial accounts ($100-500/month) are 5-10x more valuable than residential ($35-75/month) with lower churn (5-10% vs. 15-20%). Commercial requires compliance documentation (food service, healthcare, multi-family housing).

5. **Wildlife and termite specializations:** Market segments include insects (42.4% of market), termites (fastest growing segment), rodents, and wildlife. Termite inspections require different documentation. Wildlife removal requires specialized licensing.

6. **Upsell from one-time to recurring:** The conversion pipeline from emergency one-time calls to recurring contracts is critical. Industry data suggests ~40% of one-time customers can convert to annual contracts with the right follow-up. Selling to existing customers succeeds 60-70% of the time vs. 5-20% for new customers.

### 3. PAIN POINTS (3 Blocks, 4 Bullets Each)

#### Pain Point 1: Route Chaos and Recurring Schedule Nightmares

**forLine:** "For pest control operators drowning in route sheets and rescheduling calls"

- **Route density collapses with growth.** When scheduling depends on memory and manual coordination, adding customers degrades every route. One technician calling in sick means reworking the entire day by hand. What took 10 minutes each morning eventually demands constant attention throughout the day.
- **Recurring services collide with emergency calls.** The biggest scheduling challenge is not recurring treatments -- it is everything else disrupting them. Emergency call-outs, new customer requests, and equipment failures all compete for technician time. Dispatcher time becomes the hidden bottleneck.
- **Seasonal surges break manual systems.** Spring brings 2-3x demand with termite swarms and ant season. Companies that manage fine at 50 customers per week cannot handle 150 without software. Staffing becomes impossible -- hiring seasonal techs means layoffs during quiet periods.
- **Paper route sheets lose money.** Spreadsheets are static -- they show what was entered, not what is happening now. Technicians waste time driving inefficient routes, appointments run late, and growth slows because the business cannot handle additional volume without more stress.

**Supporting data:** Companies using optimized routing service 21% more jobs while cutting drive time by 30%. Optimized routes yield 2+ extra jobs daily per technician. A two-technician operation moving from spreadsheets to mobile workflow cuts admin time by 30-50% in the first month.

**Sources:** FieldRoutes blog, Briostack, Fieldster, Repair-CRM

#### Pain Point 2: Technician Tracking and Chemical Documentation Gaps

**forLine:** "For pest control companies one failed audit away from fines and license suspension"

- **Chemical records are a legal obligation, not a feature.** EPA and state regulators require tracking product name, EPA registration number, active ingredients, dilution rates, quantity used, application method, device used, and applicator information. Many states mandate 7-year retention. Pen-and-paper tracking is a compliance time bomb.
- **Technicians lose data in the field.** Apps without offline mode mean technicians in crawlspaces, basements, and rural properties lose work mid-entry. ServiceTracker users report losing all input data when relaunching after a crash. Jobber users note "no offline capability" and the "risk of data loss."
- **Commercial contracts demand documentation.** Food service, healthcare, and multi-family housing accounts require detailed treatment logs for their own regulatory compliance. Losing a top-5 commercial account because your documentation is sloppy means losing $30,000+ over five years.
- **State chemical reports take hours manually.** Generating pesticide usage logs for state reporting by hand (compiling technician notes, cross-referencing products, calculating totals) consumes admin hours that could go toward growth. Software should generate these reports in clicks, not days.

**Supporting data:** 82% of pest control companies use mobile apps, yet offline reliability remains a top complaint. Chemical tracking stored digitally reduces compliance risk and audit prep time by 70%+.

**Sources:** GorillaDesk, EPA.gov, Fieldwork, PestBase, Briostack

FieldRoutes starts at $199/month and scales based on active customers, with multi-year contracts reported. GorillaDesk starts at $49/month (Basic) or $99/month (Pro) per technician schedule. **Q3: Does pest control software work offline in areas with poor cell signal?**

This is critical for pest control technicians who work in crawlspaces, basements, rural properties, and commercial buildings with dead zones. Not all software handles this well. PestPac has offline mode but users report "inconsistent performance with occasional crashes." Jobber users note "no offline capability" with "risk of data loss." All data syncs automatically when signal returns. No lost work, no re-entry, no excuses.

**Q4: What chemical tracking features should pest control software include for EPA compliance?**

Federal and state regulations require pest control companies to track: product name, EPA registration number, active ingredients, dilution rates, quantity applied, application method, device used, and certified applicator information. Many states mandate records be stored for 7 years. Your software should let technicians log this data during the job (not after), generate state chemical reports automatically, and store everything digitally for instant audit access. If you are currently tracking chemical usage on paper or in spreadsheets, you are one failed audit away from fines or license suspension. OPS builds chemical logging into the job completion workflow so technicians record treatment data as they work, not as an afterthought at the end of the day.

**Q5: How do I switch from PestPac or FieldRoutes without losing my data?**

OPS is designed for fast setup: import your customer list, set up your routes, and start running. There is no annual contract with OPS, so you can run both systems in parallel during transition with zero financial risk.
